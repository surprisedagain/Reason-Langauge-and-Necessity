Three revolutions

What do the supposed revolutions in geometry, physics/science and metaphysics have in common?

Geometry:

(p.Bxii) The true method [of geometry], so he found, was not to inspect what he discovered either in the figure, or in the bare concept of it, and from this, as it were, to red of its properties; but to bring out wehat was necessarily implied in the concepts he himself had formed a priori and had put into the figure in thw construction by which he presented it to himself. . . . what necessarily follows from what he has himslef set into it in accordance with his concept.

This supposed resemblance between the (newly proposed correct method of) metaphysics and geometry is the least strained. Here Kant is hining at the felt defectiveness of Euclid's Elements to the Euclid's elements (in a handful of key points) upon

Science: (the most tenuous of the three)

They learned that reason has insight only into that which it produces after a plan of its own, and that it must not allow itself to be kept as it were in natures leading-strings, but must itself show the way with principles of judgement based upon fixed laws, constraining nature to give answer questions of reason's own determining. Accidental observations made in obedience to no previously thought out plan can neer be made to yield a necessary law which alone reason is concerned to discover.

division between logic and metaphysics

It is well known that Kant's opinions about the prospect of further developments in logic was completely wrong

(Bviii) It is remarkable also that to the present day this logic has not been able to advance a single step, and is thus to all appearance a closed and completed body of doctrine.

The developments of Frege to bring logic up to the level of complexity required to capture recursively enumerable sets of sentences as theorems is still 100 years off.

But more importantly Kant offers to explain this completeness as due to logic being quite precisely delimited; its sole concern is to give an exhaustive exposition and a strict proof of the formal rules of all thought, whether it be a priori or empirical.

One wonders what Kant means by formal, surely not the modern view of rules specified purely in terms of the shapes and sequences of symbols without reliance unpon their meanings. Strict proof is also somewhat unclear, or at least foreign to modern logic - how might one prove an axiom of logic, or offer it any greater cfeedence than it already has? (inference to best explanation, or because of the fruitfulness of its consequences is urely not what Kant has in mind.)

hint of argument against idealism

Kant repeatedly displays his awareness of the threat of self-contradiction in asserting that we positively know of the existence of objects about which we can know nothing.

Why posit noumena? We need a positive reasom. In the introcudtion the closest we come is

(Bxxvi) But our further contention must be duly borne in mind namely that though we cannot know these objects as things themselves we must yet be in position at least think them as things in themselves; otherwise we should be landed in the absurd conclusion they can be appearance without anything that appears.

This first hint, at least, is not particularly promising, seemign to hinge as it does on a more or less definitional stop. If these things we experience are not appearances (and hence have some external/noumenal obejects that they are appearances of) then let us call them something else. The question then simply becomes what reason do we have for thinking that our ewxperienfes are appearances, and to this, as yet, Kant has ventured no answer
