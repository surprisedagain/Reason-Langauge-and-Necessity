overview

According to Quine empirical theories are connected with their con/infirmation conditions via observation sentences. There are however two fundamental defects about Quine's account of observation sentences and how they provide this connection.

The first concerns Quine's claim that the sentences providing this connection must have fixed stimulus meanings. While this defect has never received attention such rigid connections would preclude theories from being able to issue testable predictionsit would, if consistently followed through, preclude theories from having empirical content at all. On the other hand, once noted, this defect is easily remedied and in that sense not a fundamental problem.

The second concerns intersubjective stimulus identity. The account of Word and Object requires intersubjectively identical stimuli in order to identify observation sentences, The body surface, or the impingements upon sensory nerves, are not intersubjectively identifiable for the simple rerason that different people have differently shaped (and weighted and sensitive) bodies and different distributions of nerves. Quine appears to have been well aware of this problem and has issued several attempts ***

For the purposes of identifying the con/infirmation conditions preserved by good translation the problem can be resolved by choosing a more distant, though highly artificial epistemic_bottleneck. For the purpose of a schematic account of how langauge is actually learnt and the kind of evidence that actual language learners/teachers rely upon a somewhat more complex account of the evidential basis for translation is required.
