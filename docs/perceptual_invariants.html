One way of protecting the traditional accounts of reasoning from the discovery that our sensations and thoughts do not instantiate the same properties as the objects they represent is to seek for some complex property or invariant that is instantiated by external objects, sensations and thoughts about those objects. In principle such invariants are not hard to construct, though the technical details would, of course, horrendously complex and discoverable only with the aid of onerous scientific investigation (as with most genuine discoveries). Such a property might be something like Is a surface which primarily reflects photons with a wavelength of between 650 and 700nm, or is an area of the retina or is a section of the retina in which L cone cells are predominantly firing at such and such a rate, or is a section of the visual cortex in which such and such neural activation pattern is occuring. The extravagant dissimilarity between external object surfaces, retinal cone cell firing patterns, and patterns of neural activation in the visual cortex ensures that there will be few, if any, objects that fall into the wrong clause of such a disjunctive predicate and hence that, in principle a complex predicate can be constructed which is true of just the requisite tuples of objects and processes involved in the causal chain from distal stimuli, via proximal stimuli and sensations to beliefs about the distal stimuli.

Although it is quite plausible that there is some invariant that is true for each property*though it is unclear if that invariant is projectible, it is far from clear if that invariant can be determined without relying upon the content of the relevant representational states/objects. In particular perceptual errors present a major difficulty for determining the correct invariants. To borrow an example from Fodor*Psychosemantics, Chapters 2 and 3 , if one mistakes a poorly glimpsed cow on a dark night for a horse, the percept (or whatever it is that represents what one has seen) will be a representation of a horse and not that of a cow. As Fodor goes on, at length, to argue absolutely convincingly, the prospect of deliniating some class of normal (or ideal) conditions under which perceptions do not go wrong is hopeless.
