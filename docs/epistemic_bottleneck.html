epistemic bottleneck

short

We shall spend a great deal of time talking about evidence and how it relates to contentive(/representational/semantically evaluable) things such as statememts and theories. Let us clarify just what we count as evidence.

rough notes

does not reuqire a position on justification internalism/externalism

main

We know of some objects only through their effects on other objects to which we have comparatively direct epistemic access. Stars too faint to be seen by the naked eye, for example, are known to us only through their effects on photographic plates, the antennae of radio telescopes and other astronomical equipment. I propose to call this kind of epistemic dependency an epistemic bottleneck. We shall speak of the objects, like faint stars, which are known only indirectly as being on the far side of the relevant bottleneck, and the objects through which they are known as being in the relevant bottleneck.

Some empiricists (and Kant, in his way) have thought there must be special objects, sometimes dubbed "sense-data", that are known of directly and infallibly; and that all the other objects we know of, are known only through their effects on these sense-data. Quine offered a radically different account of the evidence upon which our theories are ultimately based. Rejecting hope of any knowledge more certain than scientific theories based upon empirical investigation, he availed himself unashamedly of the empirical hypothesis that we receive all our information about the world via the firings of our sensory receptors, and took stimuli to be the ultimate evidence upon which our all our theories are based. Unlike the sense-data postulated by earlier empiricists, these stimuli were not supposed to be known infallibly nor, in general, to be referred to (even covertly) in the statements for which they were evidence. Indeed, as Quine was at pains to point out, they were not even incorrigible evidence --- our stimuli might be good evidence for statements that, on reflection, we find to be false. Still, Quine held our stimuli to be the ultimate evidence on which our theories of the world are based, on the grounds that they are both an epistemic bottleneck through which all our information about the external world must flow before going on to cause our beliefs, and because they are in practice the last clear and public cause of our beliefs.

If there were some form of ultimate evidence, whether sense-data, stimuli or what have you, those objects would be in a epistemic bottleneck of which the entire world was on the far side (or in the case of stimuli, at least the world beyond their subject's body). If there is such ultimate evidence then, as Quine puts it, "The world with its quarks and chromosomes, its distant lands and spiral nebulae, is like a vast computer in a black box, forever sealed apart from its input and output registers"({local:zz.texts/quine1978.webOfBelief} p.22.1). But the views advance in this collection of notes do not rely upon the existence of such an all encompassing epistemic bottleneck, nor of such ultimate evidence. But only the more humble claim that there are some epistemic bottlenecks or other at some points in the flow of information to us from the objects of which we have knowledge. Even if there are no clear input and output registers, and what is taken as "ultimate evidence" depends on context and passing interests, that is still compatible with the existence of epistemic bottlenecks, and hence still compatible with the arguments on offer here.
