This is the oldest exemplar of deductive reasoning in the western tradition*While the claims involved were certainly known in other cultures (see local:zz.texts/ancientChineseMathematics.pdf) I am unaware of any other tradition in which general deductive arguments were presented to justify such claims, or considered as objects of investigation or interest in their own right. In it Socrates teaches a hitherto ignorant slave boy how to construct a square that is double the size of a given square.

At first the boy mistakenly believes that the method to double the size of a square is to double the length of each of the sides.
i.e. that the solid blue square below is double the area of the red one.

Socrates, however, convinces the boy that this new square will in fact be four times as large as the original. Then Socrates shows him that the correct method is to draw the square based on the diagonal of the original.
i.e. that the true double of the red square is in fact the dashed blue square below

a further mystery of irrational numbers

One of the lovely features of Plato's dialogues is how many levels they work on simultaeneously. As is well known, each of the characters Socrates interrogates is a manifestation of the sort of personality that is attracted to the doctrines they espouse. hence there is a sense both that these issues touch upon each individuals whole approach to life and not merely their intellectual beliefs in isolation.

In the case of the Meno, this layering applies to the geometrical argument as well. The tyro geometrician can appreciate the strength of Socrates argument and count themselves knowledgeable for being able to understand Socrates prrof that the square on the diagonal is double the size of the original (see the second image above as above)

But there is a deeper mystery concealed within Socrates example. At one point Socrates asks the boy of the length of the sides of this doubled square

Soc: Then the line which forms the side of eight feet ought to be more than this

line of two feet, and less than the other of four feet?

Boy: It ought. Soc: Try and see if you can tell me how much it will be.

If we set the length of each side of the original square to 1, then the length of each side of the dashed blue square (the true double in area) is the square root of two.
